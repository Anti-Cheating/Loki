The answer lands too clean. You ask a hard, open-ended question, and three seconds later the candidate delivers a tidy, well-structured response — better organized than anything else they have said, in a slightly flatter tone. Their screen share looks normal. Nothing is obviously wrong. But something is off, and now you have to decide what to do with a feeling you cannot yet prove.

That gap — between what you sense and what you can show — is where most hiring teams are stuck right now. In an analysis of 19,368 interviews, Fabric found that [38.5% of candidates showed cheating behavior and 61% of those cheaters scored above the pass threshold, meaning they would have advanced undetected](https://fabrichq.ai/blogs/state-of-ai-interview-cheating-in-2026-insights-from-19-368-interviews). At the same time, a Checkr survey of 3,000 hiring managers found that [59% suspect candidates of using AI to misrepresent themselves, but only 19% are confident their process would actually catch it](https://checkr.com/resources/articles/hiring-hoax-manager-survey-2025). Suspicion is everywhere. Proof is rare. This is a playbook for closing that gap without wronging an honest person.

## First rule: a suspicion is a hypothesis, not a verdict

Before you do anything, slow down. A single odd moment is not evidence of cheating — it is a question you have not answered yet. The math here is unforgiving. When real cheating is a minority of your pool, even an accurate detector produces a pile of [false positives that can outnumber true positives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_rate_fallacy). The cautionary tale is Vanderbilt, which disabled Turnitin's AI detector after noting that a [claimed 1% false-positive rate, applied across roughly 75,000 papers, would wrongly flag about 750 students](https://www.vanderbilt.edu/brightspace/2023/08/16/guidance-on-ai-detection-and-why-were-disabling-turnitins-ai-detector/).

So hold two things at once: candidates really do use AI to cheat, and your gut can be wrong about which candidate did. The goal is not to catch someone. It is to find out what actually happened. Treat the rest of this as a verification process, not a prosecution.

## Step 1: Do not accuse in the moment

The worst move is to stop the interview and say "are you using AI right now?" You cannot un-ring that bell. If you are wrong, you have insulted a strong candidate and probably lost them. If you are right, you have tipped them off and they will simply close the tool.

What to do live instead:

- **Keep your face neutral and keep going.** Do not signal suspicion. You want more data, and a candidate who thinks they are safe behaves normally.
- **Make a quiet note of the timestamp.** Which question, roughly what time. You will want to line this up against evidence later.
- **Change the shape of the conversation.** Shift from questions AI answers well — "walk me through your approach to X" — toward ones it answers badly: specifics only they would know, and reactions to something you just said.

The moment of suspicion is for gathering, not confronting. The decision comes later, when you have more than a feeling.

## Step 2: Verify with follow-up questions

Follow-ups are your single most powerful tool, and they cost nothing. AI assistants are excellent at first answers and weak at the second and third layer, because those depend on the candidate's actual memory and reasoning, not a generated paragraph. Probe in three directions:

![Nested speech-bubble shapes, smooth grey-indigo on the outside and fragmented accent indigo inside, showing a confident answer thinning out under deeper follow-up questioning.](/marketing/suspect-candidate-using-ai/probing.webp)

- **Go deeper.** "You said you cut latency by rearchitecting the queue — what did you try first that did not work?" Real experience has dead ends and detours. Generated answers are suspiciously linear.
- **Go specific and personal.** "Who pushed back on that decision, and how did you handle it?" Tools can invent a plausible story, but they stumble when the follow-up demands a concrete, human detail that has to stay consistent.
- **Go sideways off something they just said.** Reference an exact phrase they used thirty seconds ago and ask them to expand. A candidate reading answers has to re-feed context to their tool; you will often hear the lag, or a subtle mismatch between the polished answer and the shaky elaboration.

The tell is not the pause itself — thinking takes time, and nerves are real. The tell is the *shape*: a fluent, structured first answer followed by a thin, evasive, or contradictory second one. When depth collapses the instant you go off-script, that is signal. When a candidate rambles happily into messy specifics, that is usually a good sign.

## Step 3: Read the timeline for convergence, not a gotcha

If you use a live integrity tool, this is where it earns its place — and where it is most often misused. One flag is not proof. A paste spike, a focus switch, an unusual answer cadence: alone, each has an innocent explanation. Together, timestamped and lined up with the moment your follow-up fell apart, they become a pattern you can actually reason about.

That is the whole discipline of [reading an integrity timeline](/resources/reading-integrity-timeline): you are looking for several independent signals converging on the same moment, not a single dramatic red mark. A paste event that coincides with an off-screen glance and an answer that suddenly outpaces everything before it is worth a closer look. A lone flag in an otherwise clean session is worth almost nothing. If you have not yet mapped the behaviors to their innocent counterparts, our [field guide to interview cheating red flags](/resources/interview-cheating-red-flags) pairs each signal with the honest explanation it usually has.

The point of the timeline is not to hand you a verdict. It is to tell you *where to look* so the conversation can tell you what it means.

## Step 4: Decide — advance, re-interview, or reject

Once the interview is over, sort what you have into one of three buckets. Be honest about which one you are actually in.

![A single luminous path splitting into three diverging routes — one continuing bright indigo, one looping back, one fading to grey — representing advance, re-interview, or reject.](/marketing/suspect-candidate-using-ai/decision.webp)

- **Advance.** Follow-ups held up, specifics were rich and consistent, and there is no converging evidence. A single soft signal with a clean explanation is not a reason to torpedo a good candidate. Move them forward.
- **Re-interview.** This is the right call far more often than people think. You have real doubt but not proof — a shaky follow-up, one or two flags, an answer that did not sit right. A short, live, structured re-interview (ideally with a different interviewer, more real-time back-and-forth, and a small work-sample or collaborative problem) resolves most of these cleanly. Structured interviews are among the [most valid predictors of job performance](https://doi.org/10.1037/apl0000994), and a redesigned second round is far more diagnostic than a rushed accusation. It is also fair: it gives an honest candidate a clean shot and gives a dishonest one a much harder problem to fake.
- **Reject.** Reserve this for convergent, timestamped evidence *plus* a follow-up performance that collapsed — not a vibe. And even here, decline on the merits ("the depth we needed was not there in the technical discussion") rather than on an accusation you cannot fully substantiate.

Notice that "confront and reject on suspicion" is not on the list. Suspicion routes to re-interview, not rejection.

## If you do raise it, do it fairly

Sometimes the evidence is strong enough that you want to give the candidate a chance to respond — and you should, because a flag can have an explanation you did not anticipate. Do it as a genuine question, not a trap.

- **Describe, do not accuse.** "In the second half, the answers changed character and a few things did not line up when I dug in. Can you help me understand that?" gives them room to explain a disability accommodation, a second-language pause, or a tool you did not know was allowed.
- **Keep a human in the decision.** Never auto-reject on a score. Regulators expect it: the EU AI Act requires [human oversight that guards against over-relying on a system's output](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/14/), and GDPR gives candidates the right to [contest a solely automated decision](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-22-gdpr/). The employer owns the decision, not the tool.
- **Watch for the false positive.** GPT text detectors have wrongly flagged large shares of writing from non-native English speakers; live signals can misfire on nerves, accents, or a bad connection. If a signal can fire on something other than cheating, weight it accordingly and let the candidate speak.

## Protect the honest majority

Most candidates are not cheating, and a heavy-handed process punishes them first. Tell candidates up front that integrity signals are active and what "allowed" looks like — many people do not know where the line sits between practicing with AI beforehand and using it live. Clear rules deter the opportunists and reassure everyone else. The measure of a good integrity process is not how many people it catches. It is how confidently it clears the ones who did nothing wrong.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should you confront a candidate you suspect is using AI in the interview?

Not in the moment, and not as an accusation. Keep the interview going, gather more with follow-up questions, and decide afterward. If the evidence is strong, raise it as a neutral, describe-what-you-saw question that lets them explain — not a "gotcha." Most suspicions should route to a re-interview, not a confrontation.

### How can you tell if a candidate is using ChatGPT during a live interview?

There is no single tell. Watch for a polished, structured first answer that collapses when you ask a specific follow-up, a consistent lag before responses, or eyes reading off to the side. Behavioral signs are only suggestive — pauses and nerves are normal. Converging, timestamped signals plus a weak follow-up performance are far more reliable than any one behavior.

### Can you reject a candidate for using AI in an interview?

You can decline any candidate, but reject on the merits and on evidence, not on a hunch. If follow-ups held up and evidence is thin, advance them. If you have real but unproven doubt, re-interview. Reserve rejection for convergent evidence, keep a human in the decision, and document what you observed rather than labeling the person a cheater.

### Is using AI in a job interview cheating?

It depends on your stated rules. Using AI to prepare is normal and fine; feeding live questions to a hidden tool and reading back answers is misrepresentation. The problem is that many candidates do not know where your line is, so publish it. A clear policy makes "cheating" a definition everyone agreed to, not a judgment call after the fact.

### What follow-up questions catch AI-assisted answers?

The ones that demand memory and specificity: "What did you try first that failed?", "Who disagreed and how did you handle it?", and questions that reference an exact phrase the candidate used moments ago. AI produces strong first answers and weak second layers. When depth evaporates the instant you go off-script, that is your signal.

## The calm version is the strong version

Suspicion is uncomfortable, and the instinct is to either ignore it or pounce. Neither is fair or accurate. Verify quietly, probe hard, read the evidence for convergence, and route doubt into a re-interview instead of an accusation. You will catch more of the people who are actually cheating and clear more of the people who are not.
