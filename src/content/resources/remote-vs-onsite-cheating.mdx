The industry has quietly picked a side. Faced with a wave of AI-assisted interview cheating, [72.4% of recruiting leaders said they are now conducting interviews in person to combat fraud](https://www.computerworld.com/article/4044734/to-counter-ai-cheating-companies-bring-back-in-person-job-interviews.html), and Google, Cisco, and McKinsey have all reinstated in-person rounds. The reasoning is intuitive: put the person in a room and the hidden overlay disappears. But almost nobody asks the follow-up question. Does flying candidates in actually close the gap, or does it just move the problem somewhere you stop looking?

This is a comparison worth doing carefully, because "remote vs onsite" is really two different questions wearing one coat. Cheating in interviews is not a single behavior. It is at least two, and each format is weak to a different one.

## Two different cheats, not one

Lump everything under "cheating" and you will make bad decisions. Separate it into two threats and the map gets clearer.

![Two abstract green icons side by side: a chat-overlay feeding thin lines into a call window for real-time AI assistance, and offset semi-transparent face outlines for identity fraud.](/marketing/remote-vs-onsite-cheating/threats.webp)

- **Real-time AI assistance.** The real candidate, being themselves, feeding questions to a hidden assistant and reading back polished answers. This is now overwhelmingly software. Across 19,368 live interviews tracked between July 2025 and January 2026, [38.5% of candidates were flagged for AI-cheating behavior, and 61% of those still scored above the pass threshold](https://fabrichq.ai/blogs/state-of-ai-interview-cheating-in-2026-insights-from-19-368-interviews). Dedicated assistants like Cluely and Interview Coder made up 45% of cases, with ChatGPT and Gemini voice mode another 34%.
- **Identity fraud.** A different person than the one you evaluate — a proxy sitting the interview, a stolen resume, or an AI-generated face on the call. This is the impersonation and deepfake problem, and it is growing fast enough that [Gartner projects 1 in 4 candidate profiles will be fake by 2028](https://www.hrdive.com/news/fake-job-candidates-ai/757126/).

These two cheats do not respond to the same fix. Onsite is very good against one of them and does almost nothing about the other. That is the whole reason "just go onsite" feels decisive and isn't.

## Where remote interviews are genuinely weak

No point being defensive about it: remote is the softer target for both threats, and pretending otherwise helps no one.

For real-time AI assistance, the remote setup is close to ideal for a cheater. A second monitor, a phone propped below the webcam, or a transparent overlay app sits outside the shared screen and outside your line of sight. The tools are built specifically to be invisible during a video call. More than [1 in 5 job seekers say they have used AI in real time during a live interview](https://resumegenius.com/blog/job-hunting/job-seeker-insights-report-2026) — that is self-reported, so the true number is almost certainly higher.

For identity fraud, remote removes the physical checks that used to happen for free. Nobody sees a driver's license at the front desk. The face on the call can be a real-time deepfake, and the voice can be a proxy piped through. The most-cited example is the North Korean IT-worker schemes that the U.S. Department of Justice has spent the last two years dismantling — real operatives, stolen identities, AI-assisted video, hired into remote roles at hundreds of companies. Remote hiring made that possible at scale.

So yes: if you do nothing, remote is easier to cheat. The mistake is assuming the only response is to abandon it.

## Where onsite interviews are genuinely weak

Onsite is strong exactly where remote is weak. A person in your building can't run a hidden overlay you can't see, and they can't be a deepfake. Impersonation collapses when you check ID at reception. If your entire threat model is identity fraud, onsite is a real fix.

But onsite has its own soft spots, and they are the ones the "go back in-person" crowd tends to skip.

- **It doesn't cover most of your funnel.** Very few teams interview every applicant onsite. The phone screen, the recruiter call, the take-home, the first technical round — all still remote, all still exposed. Onsite typically protects the final round, after you have already spent real time on candidates who cleared the vulnerable early stages using exactly the tools you were worried about.
- **Take-homes and pre-work are wide open.** An onsite final says nothing about the coding challenge someone completed at home with an AI pair-programmer. If that artifact carries weight in your decision, onsite hasn't closed the gap; it has only added a clean-looking final layer on top of a compromised one.
- **It shrinks and skews your pool.** Requiring travel filters out strong remote candidates, people with caregiving constraints, and anyone outside commuting distance. You are trading a detection problem for a selection problem, and the second one is often the more expensive.
- **It is slow and costly.** Coordinating travel adds days to the loop and real money per candidate. In a competitive market, the good people you actually want have taken another offer by the time your onsite is scheduled.
- **Determined cheating still leaks in.** A phone in a pocket, a bathroom break to check notes, a memorized answer to a leaked question. Onsite raises the effort required; it does not make cheating impossible.

Onsite converts a hard, scalable problem into an expensive, smaller one. That can be worth it for a specific high-trust final round. As a blanket policy, it is an overcorrection.

## Why "just go onsite" is a partial fix

Put the two threats and the two formats side by side and the honest verdict is: onsite is a strong answer to identity fraud and a weak, expensive answer to real-time AI assistance across a full pipeline.

![A horizontal hiring funnel where only the final, rightmost stage sits inside a green protective outline while the earlier, wider remote stages stay dimmed and unguarded.](/marketing/remote-vs-onsite-cheating/coverage-gap.webp)

The thing is, the 38.5% figure above is mostly the AI-assistance threat, not impersonation — and that is the one onsite handles worst outside the single room you can supervise. You can't run every screen, every take-home, and every early round in person. So the teams going all-in on onsite are spending the most money to solve the smaller of their two problems, while the larger one keeps flowing through every remote stage they still, necessarily, run.

There is also a quieter cost. Reverting to in-person as a fraud measure treats every candidate as a suspect who has to prove they are real by showing up. Most of your applicants are honest people who would rather interview from home. Designing your entire process around the dishonest minority punishes the majority you are trying to hire. Fairness and rigor are not opposites here, but a blanket onsite mandate quietly sacrifices the first for a partial version of the second.

## What actually closes the gap — in either format

The better framing is not remote vs onsite. It is: for each stage, what threat am I exposed to, and what is the cheapest honest way to close it?

- **Design interviews that AI can't ace for you.** Work samples tied to your real environment, role- and resume-specific questions, and adaptive follow-ups where you dig into the last answer. A hidden assistant is great at generic questions and useless when you interrupt and probe a specific claim. This is the highest-leverage move and it costs nothing. - **Verify identity early and lightly.** A brief live conversation tying the person to their application catches most impersonation before you invest hours — without making anyone board a plane. This is the identity-fraud fix that keeps your pipeline remote.
- **Read live signals where the eye can't keep up.** For the AI-assistance threat, the answer isn't lockdown or an onsite mandate — it is objective signals read from the candidate's machine, with consent. - **Keep a human on the final call.** A risk signal is a reason to ask a sharper question, never an automatic rejection. ## Frequently asked questions

### Is it easier to cheat in a remote interview than an onsite one?

If you do nothing, yes. Remote lets a candidate hide a second screen or an overlay the camera never sees, and it removes the physical ID check that catches impersonation. But that gap closes with process design, early identity verification, and consent-based live detection — none of which require making everyone travel.

### Does going back to in-person interviews stop AI cheating?

Partly. Onsite is strong against impersonation and hidden overlays in the room you supervise, but it doesn't cover phone screens, take-homes, or early technical rounds, which stay remote and exposed. Since most AI-assisted cheating happens in those earlier stages, an onsite final round often protects the smallest, latest slice of your funnel at the highest cost.

### What is onsite interviewing actually weak to?

Cost, speed, and pool size, mostly. Requiring travel filters out strong remote candidates and adds days and expense to every loop, while determined cheating — a phone in a pocket, a memorized leaked answer, an AI-assisted take-home — still leaks through. Onsite raises the effort bar; it does not eliminate the problem.

### How common is candidate impersonation and deepfake interview fraud?

In a Gartner survey of 3,000 candidates, [6% admitted to interview fraud by posing as someone else or having someone pose for them](https://www.hrdive.com/news/fake-job-candidates-ai/757126/), and Gartner projects 1 in 4 candidate profiles will be fake by 2028. Self-reported admissions understate the real rate, which is why lightweight identity verification early in the process matters more than it used to.

### Can you detect AI cheating without forcing everyone back onsite?

Yes. Consent-first tools read device-level signals — app and screen context, paste behavior, voice structure — during a normal video call and flag when a hidden assistant is active, without a browser lockdown or heavy install. Paired with interviews designed to resist AI and a human making the final decision, remote pipelines can be harder to game than onsite ones.
