A live integrity tool hands you two things: a score and a timeline of flagged moments. Used well, they make you a sharper interviewer. Used badly, they turn you into a prosecutor working from one data point. The difference is entirely in how you read them.

## One flag is not proof

Start with the math, because it is unforgiving. When real cheating is rare in your pool, [false positives outnumber true positives even with a very accurate detector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_rate_fallacy) — a consequence of the base-rate fallacy [first described by Tversky and Kahneman](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.185.4157.1124). A vivid example: Vanderbilt disabled Turnitin's AI detector after noting that even a [1% false-positive rate, across its 75,000 papers, meant about 750 students wrongly flagged](https://www.vanderbilt.edu/brightspace/2023/08/16/guidance-on-ai-detection-and-why-were-disabling-turnitins-ai-detector/). The detectors themselves are shaky: [OpenAI shut down its own classifier for low accuracy](https://techcrunch.com/2023/07/25/openai-scuttles-ai-written-text-detector-over-low-rate-of-accuracy/), and GPT detectors [misflag 61% of essays from non-native English speakers as AI](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10382961/). Treat any single flag as a question, never an answer.

## Read the signals together, not one "gotcha"

The reliable read comes from convergence. Selection science is explicit that you should [weigh converging lines of evidence rather than a single indicator](https://www.siop.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/Considerations-and-Recommendations-for-the-Validation-and-Use-of-AI-Based-Assessments-for-Employee-Selection-January-2023.pdf). The test-security field says the same thing about its own anomaly data: [it "does not necessarily confirm cheating" and "must be supplemented with other information"](https://www.testpublishers.org/assets/Using%20Data%20Forensics%20to%20Detect%20Potential%20Fraud.pdf). A paste spike on its own means little. A paste spike that lines up with a focus switch, an off-screen gaze, and an answer that suddenly outpaces everything before it is a pattern worth a closer look.

## Read the timeline in context

A flag is a timestamp plus what was happening around it. Your job is to separate normal behavior from a real pattern.

- **A glance away is not a tell.** Breaking eye contact is how people think — [averting your gaze actually improves recall and accuracy on hard questions](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/BF03211385). A pause to think looks nothing like sustained reading off a fixed line.
- **A flag can be an artifact.** Automated proctoring famously flags some groups far more than others with [no underlying difference in cheating](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2022.881449/full). If a signal can fire on appearance or accent, weight it accordingly.
- **Read it like a security analyst reads telemetry.** Events that look benign alone can matter together — judge each flag against a baseline and across time, not as a single snapshot.

## Keep due process

Once you act on a flag, the law expects a human to stay in charge. The EU AI Act requires [human oversight that guards against "automation bias" — over-relying on the system's output](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/14/), and the GDPR gives candidates the right to [contest a solely automated decision and obtain human intervention](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-22-gdpr/). Practically: do not auto-reject on a score. Give the candidate a chance to explain — a flag can reflect a disability or a tool you did not anticipate, which is why the EEOC and DOJ both stress [accommodations and human review](https://www.ada.gov/resources/ai-guidance/). And remember the employer, not the vendor, owns the decision and the liability.

## Turn evidence into a fair decision

NIST frames it cleanly: an AI system can defer to a human or serve as ["an additional opinion,"](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/nist.ai.100-1.pdf) and the human-AI loop can amplify bias if you let the score do the deciding. So treat the integrity score as one input alongside the strongest predictor you have — the [structured interview, validity ~.42](https://doi.org/10.1037/apl0000994) — and watch for confirmation bias once a flag appears. The score tells you where to look. The conversation tells you what it means. You make the call.

That is the whole point of a timeline: not to accuse, but to give you evidence you can actually reason about. [Trueyy](/features) is built to surface those signals in context, with the timestamp, so the decision stays yours.
