You bought exam proctoring, pointed it at a live interview, and something felt off. The candidate needed their camera, a screen share, an IDE, and a browser tab for the coding pad. The proctoring tool wanted to force full screen, kill the tabs, and lock the clipboard. Those two things cannot both happen. So you turned the lockdown off, and now you own an exam tool that is not proctoring your interview at all.

If that is where you are, you are not doing it wrong. You are using a lock where the job needs a smoke detector. Exam proctoring is good software aimed at the wrong shape of problem. This guide walks through the actual interview proctoring alternatives, what each category is genuinely for, and a plain set of criteria for picking one, without the vendor-listicle padding that treats every tool as interchangeable.

## Why exam proctoring is the wrong shape for interviews

Start with the difference that breaks everything else. An exam is one person answering fixed questions inside a window the institution controls. A live interview is a two-way conversation on Zoom, Google Meet, or Microsoft Teams, on a machine you do not own, where the person is supposed to talk, share a screen, whiteboard, and think out loud.

That is the whole "exam proctoring vs interview" story in one line: exam proctoring controls the environment, and in an interview you have no environment to control.

Every lockdown feature assumes something an interview cannot give it:

- **A single window to seal.** Interviews need several surfaces open at once. Force-full-screen and disable-new-tabs break the interview itself.
- **A machine you own.** An institution can mandate a locked browser on a lab machine. In hiring you are a guest on someone's personal laptop for 45 minutes. Demanding deep lockdown of a candidate's whole computer is impractical and a bad candidate experience.
- **The threat living in the browser.** The tools candidates reach for now are invisible AI overlays like Cluely and Interview Coder. They render below the screen-share layer and feed answers in real time. A browser lockdown never sees them, because they never touch the browser.

The pressure to solve this is real and rising. In one 2025 survey of 3,617 professionals, [20% admitted secretly using AI during interviews and 55% said it has become the new norm](https://www.teamblind.com/blog/1-in-5-u-s-professionals-secretly-use-ai-during-job-interviews/). A separate 2026 report found [22% of candidates use AI during real-time interviews](https://www.newsweek.com/job-seekers-using-ai-during-interviews-11911462), not just to polish a resume beforehand. So the instinct to reach for the proctoring tool you already own makes sense. It just does not fit.

## The false-positive tax you inherit

There is a second reason to think twice before repurposing exam proctoring: the accuracy problem comes along for the ride.

Automated proctoring flags behavior that looks suspicious, and honest people trip those flags constantly. Someone looks up to think. Someone pauses. Someone speaks non-native English. The scoring has to be overcautious, which means it produces false positives by design. Industry guidance is blunt about the scale: [a 1% false positive rate across a large institution could generate thousands of wrongful accusations a year](https://integrityadvocate.com/resource-center/blog/ai-only-proctoring-has-a-false-positive-problem-heres-what-that-costs-your-program/), and Stanford researchers cited in that same piece found over half of writing samples from non-native English speakers were misclassified as AI-generated by automated detectors.

In an exam program, a wrongful flag triggers an academic-integrity review. In hiring, a wrongful flag can quietly cost someone a job they earned. That asymmetry is why any interview proctoring alternative you pick has to be judged as much on how it handles false positives as on what it catches.

## The proctoring alternatives, by category

"Proctoring alternative" is not one product. It is four categories that solve four different problems. Buy the wrong one and you will be back here in a month.

![Four abstract category tiles on a dark magenta background showing exam lockdown, code-similarity brackets, an ID face-outline, and a glowing magenta live audio waveform marked as the interview fit.](/marketing/live-interview-proctoring-alternatives/categories.webp)

### Exam and assessment proctoring

Proctorio, Honorlock, and the proctor modes in HackerRank and CodeSignal live here. Browser lockdown, webcam and screen recording, a suspicion score for a human to review afterward. This category is excellent, for exams. If your process includes a timed skills test or a certification inside a browser or LMS, keep it. The mistake is only in aiming it at the live conversation. We wrote a full, no-strawman breakdown of that boundary in [Trueyy vs Proctorio](/resources/trueyy-vs-proctorio).

### Code plagiarism and similarity checkers

MOSS, Codequiry, and the code-similarity features inside assessment platforms compare submitted code against a corpus and against other candidates. They are the right tool for take-home and async coding tasks, where the question is whether the submitted work is original. They are after-the-fact by nature and say nothing about what happened live on the call. Our [Trueyy vs HackerRank](/resources/trueyy-vs-hackerrank) comparison covers where assessment-time checks end and live-interview monitoring begins.

### Identity and video verification

This category answers "is this the person we think it is." Deepfake and proxy detection, ID checks, liveness. It matters more than it used to: in a survey of 3,000 managers, [1 in 3 suspected or confirmed that someone other than the listed candidate had joined an interview](https://www.newsweek.com/most-managers-suspect-ai-fraud-in-hiring-survey-10477225). But identity verification confirms who is on the call, not whether that person is getting real-time AI help. It is a complement to live monitoring, not a substitute.

### Live-interview signal detection

This is the category built for the actual problem: someone getting real-time AI assistance during a two-way conversation you do not control. Instead of sealing a window, it reads the signals a hidden assistant leaves behind, live, beside the call. Trueyy is in this category, and it is where the rest of this guide focuses.

## How to choose a proctoring alternative for interviews

Skip the feature checklists for a moment and answer these first. They sort the categories faster than any comparison table.

- **What are you protecting?** A timed test, a take-home, or a live conversation. Different answers point at different categories. Only the last one is an interview problem.
- **Do you own the machine?** If yes, lockdown is on the table. In hiring the answer is almost always no, which rules out anything that depends on controlling the candidate's environment.
- **Real-time or after-the-fact?** Plagiarism checks and recording review happen later. Live interview monitoring has to score risk during the call, while you can still ask a follow-up question.
- **What surface does it watch?** Browser-only tools miss overlays that never touch the browser. Device-level signal reading sees app and screen activity, paste patterns, and voice. Ask specifically whether it catches Cluely and Interview Coder.
- **Coverage: blocklist or signatures?** A keyword blocklist breaks the moment a tool renames itself. Structural output signatures recognize the shape of machine-generated help regardless of the tool's name.
- **How does it handle false positives?** Does it weigh multiple signals together, or fire on one? Is there a human in the final decision, or does the software hand down a verdict?
- **What is the privacy posture?** Is video stored on a vendor's servers? Is there biometric identification? Lighter is usually better for a job candidate on a personal device.

That last cluster matters because the confidence gap is the real problem you are buying against. In that same manager survey, [59% suspected AI misrepresentation while only 19% were confident their process would catch a fraudulent applicant](https://www.newsweek.com/most-managers-suspect-ai-fraud-in-hiring-survey-10477225). Closing that gap without manufacturing false accusations is the whole job.

## A word on fairness and false positives

Whatever you pick, the goal is a level field for honest candidates, not surveillance. A nervous candidate, a non-native English speaker, or someone who pauses to think can trip a single signal without doing anything wrong. That is exactly why single-signal, browser-only lockdown ports so badly to interviews, and why any tool worth buying weighs multiple signals together, keeps a person in the loop, and asks for consent up front. A tool that produces false positives you act on blindly is worse than no tool at all. Be transparent, be consistent across candidates, and be generous with the benefit of the doubt.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the difference between exam proctoring and interview proctoring?

Exam proctoring controls the environment: it locks the browser, disables tabs and clipboard, and records the session while a person answers fixed questions. Interview proctoring reads signals during an open conversation you do not control, on a machine you do not own, where the candidate is supposed to talk and share their screen. One seals a window; the other listens for the tells of hidden AI assistance in a two-way call.

### Is there a proctoring alternative that works for live remote interviews?

Yes. Live-interview signal detection is a distinct category from exam proctoring. Instead of locking the browser, it sits beside your Zoom, Meet, or Teams call and reads device-level signals in real time to flag likely AI assistance. Trueyy is built for this specific job, with risk scored roughly every 30 seconds and a human making the final decision.

### Can you monitor a live interview without locking down the candidate's browser?

Yes, and for hiring you generally should. You are a guest on a candidate's personal machine, so deep lockdown is both impractical and a poor experience. Consent-based device-level signal reading watches for the signatures of hidden AI tools without forcing full screen, disabling tabs, or taking over the machine, which keeps the interview itself usable.

### Do proctoring tools give false positives, and how do I avoid them?

They can, especially single-signal automated ones. Reported false-positive rates are high enough that even a 1% rate at scale can produce thousands of wrongful flags, and non-native English speakers are disproportionately misclassified by automated detectors. To reduce the risk, choose a tool that combines multiple signals, keeps a human in the final decision, and treats its output as a risk timeline to investigate rather than a verdict.
