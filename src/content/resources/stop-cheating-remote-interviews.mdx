You cannot stop remote interview cheating by watching harder. The current generation of interview-assistance tools is built to be invisible during a screen share, feeding a candidate polished answers through an overlay your camera and your shared window never see. Staring more intently at someone's eyes does not fix that. The interviewers already sense the gap: in a Checkr survey of 3,000 managers, [59% suspected candidates of using AI to misrepresent themselves, but only 19% were confident their process would catch a fraudulent applicant](https://www.newsweek.com/most-managers-suspect-ai-fraud-in-hiring-survey-10477225).

So this is not a "trust your gut" article. Stopping cheating in remote interviews is a design problem with three layers: build a process where cheating does not pay, read live signals for the cases where someone tries anyway, and keep a human making the final call. Get the order right and you close most of the gap without turning honest candidates into suspects.

## Start by accepting the premise

The teams that handle this well in 2026 are not the ones with the cleverest detector. They are the ones who accepted that some candidates will reach for AI, and rebuilt their interviews so it barely helps. That reframing matters, because the scale is real: across 19,368 live interviews tracked between July 2025 and January 2026, [38.5% of candidates were flagged for AI-cheating behavior, and 61% of those cheaters still scored above the pass threshold](https://fabrichq.ai/blogs/state-of-ai-interview-cheating-in-2026-insights-from-19-368-interviews). The problem is not only that people cheat. It is that a poorly designed interview rewards it.

Detection is necessary, but it is the second move, not the first. If your interview can be aced by reading a fluent generated answer aloud, no tool will fully save you. Fix the interview first, then add signals for the determined minority. ## Layer 1: design the process so cheating does not pay

Most of your leverage is here, and none of it requires buying anything.

- **Lead with a work sample.** The single most cheat-resistant stage is a short, realistic task that mirrors the actual job, followed by a live conversation about it. Ask the candidate to walk you through their choices, then throw a curveball: change a constraint, question a decision, ask what they would do differently. AI can generate the artifact; it cannot fake a person's lived reasoning about a specific, slightly messy problem under follow-up.

- **Make questions role- and company-specific.** Generic questions ("tell me about a challenge") are exactly what a hidden assistant answers best. Tie questions to your real environment and to the specifics on the candidate's own resume. Ask for the name of the stakeholder in a conflict story, the exact number they moved a metric, why they chose one approach over the obvious alternative. AI has no memories to draw on.

- **Interrupt and probe.** Fixed scripts let a tool stay one step ahead. Adaptive interviews — where you follow the thread, dig into the last answer, and change direction — force real-time thinking that a copy-paste pipeline cannot keep up with. A three-to-five-second pause before every suddenly complete answer is a lot harder to hide when you are not asking the questions in a predictable order.

- **Verify identity early.** A brief live conversation at the top, tying the person on screen to their application, catches most impersonation before you invest hours. This is cheap and it is separate from AI-assistance detection.

- **Set a clear policy.** Decide whether AI is allowed for the role and say so out loud. For roles where directing AI well *is* the job, let them use it openly and evaluate how they prompt, critique, and correct it. For roles that need unaided judgment, state plainly that live interviews are for their own work. A clear, enforced rule beats a vague ban everyone quietly ignores.

Do only this and you have already made your interviews meaningfully harder to game. What process cannot do is see what is running on the candidate's machine.

## Layer 2: read live signals during the call

Past a certain volume, a human eye cannot keep up with software-based cheating, and the tells are easy to misread. This is where tooling earns its place — not to replace your judgment, but to attach objective signals to the exact moment they happen.

The signals worth reading during a live remote interview are:

- **App and screen context** — whether a hidden overlay or AI assistant is active on the candidate's device.
- **Keystroke and paste behavior** — a wall of text pasted in one motion reads nothing like typed-out thinking.
- **Voice and answer structure** — the flat reading cadence and templated scaffolding of a generated answer.

## Layer 3: keep human judgment in the loop

This is the layer teams most often get wrong, and it carries the highest stakes. A risk signal is a prompt to look closer, not a verdict.

An integrity program that produces false accusations is worse than no program at all. It punishes honest people, damages your employer brand, and exposes you to legal risk — while the sophisticated cheaters adapt and move on. Plenty of thoughtful, nervous, or neurodivergent candidates pause and glance away. A second monitor might be an accessibility tool. A pasted block might be notes they wrote themselves. None of those is proof of anything.

So write the rule into your process: a high risk score triggers a review, never an automatic rejection. The review is a human move — a sharper follow-up question, a second interviewer, a structured re-check of the moment that got flagged. Read an integrity report as a timeline of moments, not a single number, and weigh it against the whole conversation. Did the candidate go deep when pushed off-script? Do the specifics hold up? Is there an innocent explanation? No automated system should reject anyone on its own, and keeping a person on the final decision is also how you stay on the right side of anti-discrimination law.

## Deterrence: say it out loud

The cheapest win happens before the interview starts. Tell candidates, in writing, what your policy is and that you use real-time integrity signals during live interviews. Disclosure is not just ethical — it is a deterrent. Someone who knows structural signals are being read is far less likely to try, and honest candidates relax when the rules are clear and applied to everyone the same way. This shrinks the problem down to the small group actually trying to game you, which is exactly who your detection layer should spend its attention on.

## A simple order to roll this out

You do not need to do everything at once:

1. **This week:** write the AI policy, add a work sample, and rewrite your top questions to be specific to the role and the resume.
2. **This month:** train interviewers to probe and follow up instead of reading a fixed script, and add a two-reviewer rule so no one is rejected on a hunch.
3. **When volume outpaces the eye:** add consent-first live detection, and hold it to the standard here — structural not keyword-based, no lockdown, no video, human on the final call.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do you stop candidates from using AI in a remote interview?

You cannot force-stop it, so you make it not worth doing. Design interviews around work samples, role-specific questions, and adaptive follow-ups that AI cannot fake, disclose a clear policy, and add consent-based live detection for the minority who still try. The combination of deterrence, live signals, and human judgment closes most of the gap.

### Can you detect ChatGPT or Cluely during a live video interview?

You can detect the signals they produce. Tools that read device-level context, paste behavior, and answer structure in real time can flag when a hidden assistant is active even when it stays invisible to a screen share. Detecting by structural output signatures rather than app names matters, because tools like Cluely and Interview Coder rename themselves to dodge keyword lists.

### Do lockdown browsers or proctoring stop remote interview cheating?

Not really, for live interviews. Lockdown browsers and remote proctoring were built for timed tests, and modern interview assistants run on a second device or as an overlay that a locked browser never sees. A live conversation needs signals read from the candidate's machine and a human interpreting them, not exam-style restriction.

### How do you prevent false accusations of cheating?

Treat every signal as a reason to ask another question, never as a verdict. No single pause, glance, or flag should end a candidacy; a high risk score should route to a follow-up or a second reviewer instead. Disclose what you monitor, account for benign explanations like accessibility tools or slow connections, and keep a human on every decision.

### Should we go back to in-person interviews to stop cheating?

For some high-trust final rounds, an in-person or verified live stage is a reasonable safeguard. But scrapping remote interviewing throws away a wider, often better candidate pool to solve a problem that consent-first detection and good process already handle. Most teams get further by making remote interviews harder to game than by abandoning them.
