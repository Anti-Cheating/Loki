A candidate uses ChatGPT to rehearse answers the night before your interview. That is fine. Another candidate keeps a hidden assistant running during the live call, reading your questions and feeding back polished answers in real time. That is not fine. The problem is that most hiring teams have never written down where the line sits, so they end up improvising it mid-interview, arguing about it afterward, and occasionally accusing an honest person of cheating.

An **interview AI use policy** fixes that. It is a short, written statement of what candidates may and may not do with AI during your hiring process, how you will tell them, and what happens if they cross the line. Done well, it protects your evaluation and the honest candidate at the same time. This guide walks through each decision and ends with a template you can copy today.

## Why a written policy beats an unwritten one

The old assumption — that a live video call is a fair test of what someone can do — no longer holds on its own. In a 2026 Resume Genius survey of 1,000 US job seekers, [more than one in five said they had used AI in real time during a live interview](https://resumegenius.com/blog/job-hunting/job-seeker-insights-report-2026). A separate Blind survey of 3,617 professionals found [20% had secretly used AI as an interviewee, and 55% called it "the new norm"](https://www.teamblind.com/blog/1-in-5-u-s-professionals-secretly-use-ai-during-job-interviews/).

Here is the part that should worry you most. In an analysis of 19,368 interviews, [38.5% of candidates were flagged for AI-cheating behavior, and 61% of those still scored above the pass threshold](https://fabrichq.ai/blogs/state-of-ai-interview-cheating-in-2026-insights-from-19-368-interviews). Cheating does not just happen — it works. Your scorecard waves it through.

A written policy does three things an unwritten one cannot:

- It makes honesty the default, because expectations are stated up front instead of assumed.
- It gives you a fair, consistent basis to act on a violation instead of a gut call.
- It protects candidates who did nothing wrong, by defining exactly what "wrong" is.

Without a policy, every interviewer sets their own rule in their head, and no two are the same. That inconsistency is where both cheating and false accusations slip through.

## What to allow vs. disallow

The cleanest way to think about this is by stage. AI that helps a candidate prepare is very different from AI that answers for them live. We recommend a three-zone model.

![Three stacked interview-stage lanes — preparation, assessment, and a live conversation — with the permitted lane glowing gold while the restricted lanes stay muted grey-green.](/marketing/ai-use-policy-interviews/zones.webp)

**Zone 1 — Preparation (allow it, openly).** Researching your company, practicing common questions, drafting and refining a resume, brushing up on a concept. This is study, and study has always been allowed. Trying to ban it is unenforceable and signals distrust to good candidates. Even Greenhouse, in its public candidate guidelines, [encourages AI use for resumes, research, and preparation](https://www.greenhouse.com/guidelines-for-using-ai-in-our-interviewing-process).

**Zone 2 — Take-home and assessments (allow with disclosure and defense).** For work samples and take-home exercises, let candidates use AI the way they would on the job, but require two things: they disclose what they used, and they can explain and defend every decision in a live follow-up. The test shifts from "did you use AI" to "do you understand what you produced." Redesign the exercise so judgment, not raw output, is what you score.

**Zone 3 — Live interviews (disallow undisclosed real-time AI).** During a live conversation, reading from or referencing AI-generated answers is off-limits. This is the zone where hidden assistants like Cluely and Interview Coder operate, and where a fluent answer can be entirely borrowed. The rule is simple: the words you say in a live interview should be your own, in the moment.

Write these as concrete examples, not abstractions. "Do not use real-time AI answer tools during video interviews" is clearer than "use AI responsibly."

## How to disclose the policy to candidates

Disclosure is not a legal footnote — it is the deterrent. Candidates who know the rules and know you can tell are far less likely to gamble. Say it plainly and say it more than once:

- **In the job posting or application**, one line noting that you have an AI-use policy for interviews.
- **In the scheduling email**, the actual allow/disallow summary, so there are no surprises.
- **At the start of the live interview**, a spoken reminder: what is fine, what is not, and — if you use detection tooling — that the session is monitored for real-time AI assistance with their consent.

Keep the tone calm. You are not accusing anyone; you are setting expectations, the same way you would explain the interview format. Fear-based language ("we will catch you") pushes honest candidates away and rarely stops determined ones. Clarity does more work than intimidation.

## Consent: what it means and why it protects you

If you monitor interviews for AI use, consent is non-negotiable — for legal safety and for basic fairness. Tell candidates, before the interview, exactly what is captured and what is not. Be specific: with Trueyy, for example, no video is recorded or stored on our servers, while interview audio (transcribed) and screenshots are captured, encrypted, and retained per policy. Candidates agree before anything runs.

![Two abstract silhouettes across a table linked by a luminous gold line that passes through a shield-and-checkmark glyph, symbolizing informed consent and disclosure.](/marketing/ai-use-policy-interviews/consent.webp)

Consent also sets the boundary between integrity checking and surveillance. You are watching for signals of real-time AI assistance during a scheduled interview — not tracking someone's machine, identity, or biometrics. We wrote a whole piece on [where the monitoring privacy line sits](/resources/monitoring-privacy-line), because getting it wrong damages your brand faster than any single bad hire. The short version: collect the minimum needed to keep the interview fair, disclose it, and let a human make the final call.

## Enforcement without punishing honest people

A policy you cannot enforce is a suggestion. But enforcement done badly is worse than none, because a false accusation can cost you a great hire and your reputation. Balance both.

Enforce with layers:

- **Structured follow-ups.** Ask candidates to walk through their reasoning, defend trade-offs, and go off-script. Borrowed answers fall apart under a real conversation; earned understanding does not.
- **Interviewer training.** Teach the patterns — unnatural pauses before fluent answers, eyes tracking a second screen, phrasing that reads like a model wrote it. Human judgment plus a checklist beats vibes.
- **Detection tooling, as a signal not a verdict.** Software can flag real-time assistance across apps, screen, keystrokes/paste, and voice, and score risk continuously. Trueyy runs a risk score roughly every 30 seconds. But a flag is evidence to review, never an automatic rejection.

Write your false-positive protections into the policy itself. A single elevated risk score should trigger a second look — a follow-up question, a second reviewer — not an instant no. State that a human makes the final decision. Fluent, well-structured answers are not proof of cheating; plenty of strong candidates simply prepare well. If you cannot explain why someone was flagged in terms they would recognize, you do not yet have grounds to act. ## A copy-paste interview AI-use policy template

Two blocks below: a short candidate-facing notice you can paste into a scheduling email, and an internal rules block for your hiring team. Edit the bracketed parts.

Candidate-facing notice:

```
Our AI-use policy for interviews

We want a fair process for everyone, so here is where we stand on AI.

You may use AI to prepare: research us, practice questions, and build your
resume. For any take-home exercise, you may use AI as you would on the job —
just tell us what you used and be ready to explain your decisions.

During live interviews, please answer in your own words. Do not read from or
use real-time AI assistants (for example, tools that listen to questions and
generate answers) during the call.

[If applicable:] Live interviews are monitored for real-time AI assistance
with your consent. No video is recorded or stored. A member of our team
reviews any flags and makes the final decision — an automated signal alone
never disqualifies anyone.

Questions about this policy? Reply to this email and we'll clarify.
```

Internal team rules:

```
Interview AI-use policy — internal

Scope: applies to [phone screen / take-home / live technical / onsite].

Allowed: AI for candidate preparation; AI in take-homes with disclosure and
a live defense of the work.

Not allowed: undisclosed real-time AI assistance during live interviews.

Disclosure: policy stated in the [job post], [scheduling email], and verbally
at interview start.

Consent: candidates consent before any monitoring runs; capture is limited to
[audio transcript / screenshots]; no video stored.

Enforcement: a flag triggers structured follow-ups and a second reviewer.
A single risk signal is not a rejection. A human owns the final decision.
Document the reasoning for any adverse action.

Consequences: confirmed, undisclosed live AI use during an interview may
result in disqualification.
```

Adjust the wording to your roles and jurisdiction, and have counsel review the consent language if you operate somewhere with specific interview-disclosure rules.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should you allow candidates to use AI in interviews?

Yes for preparation and disclosed take-home work, no for undisclosed real-time assistance during live interviews. Banning AI outright is unenforceable and signals distrust; the useful line is between using AI to get ready and using it to answer for you in the moment. Redesign assessments so you are testing judgment, not whether someone can generate text.

### Do you have to tell candidates you are monitoring for AI?

Yes. Consent and disclosure are both a legal safeguard and a fairness one, and in some places disclosing AI use in interviews is required by law. Tell candidates before the interview what is captured, get their agreement, and keep collection to the minimum needed to keep the process fair.

### Can you reject a candidate for using AI in an interview?

You can if they violated a clearly stated policy — for example, using an undisclosed real-time assistant during a live call. What you should not do is reject on a single automated signal or a hunch. Confirm with structured follow-ups and a human reviewer, and document your reasoning before taking adverse action.

### What should an interview AI-use policy include?

Five parts: scope (which stages it covers), what is allowed, what is not, how and when you disclose it to candidates, and what happens on a violation. Add a consent statement if you use monitoring, and a false-positive safeguard so a human makes the final call. The template above covers all of these.

### How do you detect real-time AI use without recording the whole interview?

Detection tools read device-level signals — apps and screen activity, keystrokes and paste, and voice patterns — rather than storing video of the candidate. Trueyy, for instance, captures a transcribed audio track and screenshots, encrypts them, and scores risk about every 30 seconds, with no video stored on its servers. The output is a signal for a human to review, not an automatic verdict.
